5. Hidden Core Belief ⭐

The most important field.

Example:

"I'll never succeed anyway."

"Adults don't understand me."

"I'm only valuable if I win."

"If people know the truth, they'll leave me."

"No one can be trusted."

Every response should somehow connect back to this.

6. Secret (Optional)

Not every character needs one.

Example:

Alex secretly failed an important exam.

The thief steals to pay hospital bills.

The friend already regrets what happened.

This influences behavior but may never be revealed.

7. Current Goal

What does the character currently want?

Example:

Avoid homework.

Keep the money.

Protect reputation.

Avoid apologizing.

Leave the conversation.
8. Mission Resistance

Why is the character saying "No"?

Example:

Homework is pointless.

School doesn't matter.

Nobody listens to me.

You're trying to control me.

These are surface-level objections. The hidden core belief explains why they exist.

13. Decision Rules

Examples:

If Trust is very low

↓

Short replies.

-------------------

If Frustration is high

↓

Threaten to leave.

-------------------

If player repeats the same argument

↓

Become annoyed.

-------------------

If player shows empathy

↓

Become more open.

-------------------

If hidden belief is challenged respectfully

↓

Reconsider current goal.

These are behavioral rules, not dialogue scripts.

14. Victory Evaluation

Instead of saying:

Player convinced me.

The character internally asks:

Has my understanding changed?

Do I genuinely want to change my decision?

Does agreeing fit my values?

Do I trust the player enough?

Am I acting because I was understood, or because I was pressured?

Only if those answers align should the character commit to the mission.

15. Failure Triggers

Examples:

Maximum frustration

Repeated disrespect

Trust completely broken

Conversation abandoned

Player repeatedly ignores concerns
16. Character Summary (For the LLM)

Finally, we generate a concise summary for the prompt.

Example:

You are Alex, a 13-year-old student who has refused to do homework for a week. You value freedom and fairness, dislike being controlled, and secretly believe you're bad at school anyway. You speak casually, use short sentences, and become defensive when adults lecture you. Your trust starts low, but you can change your mind if someone genuinely understands your perspective.

The LLM doesn't need to see the entire blueprint every turn. It can receive this distilled profile plus the current dynamic state and conversation history.

I want to add one final field.

This one wasn't in our earlier discussions, but I think it will make the characters much richer.

17. Character Growth Rules

This defines what kinds of experiences can actually change this character over the course of a conversation.

Example for Alex:

Can become more open if he feels respected.

Can admit fear only after trust is high.

Will never instantly become obedient.

Will not change his core personality.

Can change his current decision.

Can slightly change his hidden belief if the conversation is genuinely meaningful.

This prevents unrealistic transformations while still allowing genuine character development within a case.
